Reading "at least", "more than", "between"

Each of these phrases turns into an inequality, and the whole game is whether the boundary number is in or out: "at least" and "at most" keep it, while "more than" and "less than" step one whole number past it. The table below shows the five patterns; the questions ask you to pin down the exact whole numbers.

The five phrases (using 5, or 5 to 8, as an example)
PhraseInequalityWhole numbers it allows
at least 5x ≥ 55, 6, 7, …
more than 5x > 56, 7, 8, …
at most 5x ≤ 5…, 3, 4, 5
less than 5x < 5…, 2, 3, 4
between 5 and 8 (inclusive)5 ≤ x ≤ 85, 6, 7, 8
count = last − first + 1

"At least" and "at most" include the boundary; "more than" and "less than" exclude it, so for whole numbers you step one past — more than 5 starts at 6, less than 10 ends at 9. To count a run of whole numbers from the first to the last (inclusive), it's last − first + 1, not last − first. And watch for a hidden range: "two-digit numbers" means 10 through 99.
