(Theoretical problem.) Suppose that Newton's method is used to solve \(f(x)=0\) with \(f(x) = e^x + e^{-x} - 2\text{.}\) Show that the function \(g(x)=x-f(x)/f'(x)\) (extended by \(g(0)=0\)) has a nonzero derivative at \(0\text{.}\) (Hence, \(0\) is not a superattracting point for Newton's method in this example.)
