The previous paragraph operates with an odd number \(n\) which is more natural in this context because trigonometric polynomials (25.1.3) normally contain an odd number of terms. But what if we are given an even number of points to interpolate through, such as 10? It is not clear whether to keep \(k=n/2\) as positive: k=[0:n/2 1-n/2:-1] or to move it to negative: k=[0:n/2-1 -n/2:-1]. Neither version works well. Their average does work; essentially, the term with \(k=n/2\) should be split into two. To avoid this complication, we stick to odd \(n\text{.}\)
