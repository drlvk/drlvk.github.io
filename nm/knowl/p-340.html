Newton's method (SectionÂ 9.1) is based on the idea of replacing a nonlinear function with its linear approximation, and solving the resulting linear equation. The linear approximation comes from the derivative. In the setting of several variables we have partial derivatives which are arranged into the Jacobian matrix. The \((i, j)\) entry of the Jacobian matrix is the derivative of the \(i\)th component of \(\mathbf F\) with respect to the \(j\)th component of \(\mathbf x\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} J = \begin{pmatrix} \partial F_1/\partial x_1 \amp \partial F_1/\partial x_2 \\ \partial F_2/\partial x_1 \amp \partial F_2/\partial x_2 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

(or more rows/columns if there are more variables).
