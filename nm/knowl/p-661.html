The above theorem is easier to prove than one might expect. If \(x\) is one of interpolation points, then both sides of (22.1.1) are equal to 0. Suppose \(x\) is not equal to any interpolation point. Then we can let

\begin{equation*} A = \frac{f(x) - p(x)}{ \prod_{k=1}^n (x-x_k) } \end{equation*}

so that \(f(x) -p(x) - A \prod_{k=1}^n (x-x_k) = 0\text{.}\) Consider the function

\begin{equation} g(t) = f(t) -p(t) - A \prod_{k=1}^n (t-x_k)\tag{22.1.2} \end{equation}

and notice that it has at least \(n+1\) roots on the interval \([a, b]\text{,}\) namely \(x, x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\text{.}\) Between any two roots of \(g\) there is a root of \(g'\text{:}\) this is Rolle's theorem. Therefore, \(g'\) has at least \(n\) roots on the interval \([a, b]\text{.}\) Repeat this argument: \(g''\) has at least \(n-1\) roots, â€¦ , \(g^{(n)}\) has at least 1 root. Call this root \(\xi\) and notice that according to (22.1.2),

\begin{equation*} g^{(n)}(\xi) = f^{(n)}(\xi) - 0 - A n! \end{equation*}

hence \(A = f^{(n)}(\xi) / n!\text{,}\) completing the proof of (22.1.1).
