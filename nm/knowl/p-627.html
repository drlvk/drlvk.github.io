We are used to seeing polynomials written in terms of the monomial basis \(x^0, x^1, \dots, x^d\text{,}\) that is, as a linear combination of the monomials. But this basis is ill-suited for numerical computations away from the point \(x=0\text{.}\) For example, consider the simple polynomial \(p(x) = (x-7)^{15}\text{.}\) If we expand it in the monomial basis and then try to compute its values on the interval \([6, 8]\text{,}\) the result will be disappointing.
